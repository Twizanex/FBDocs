Guide: Creating Open Graph Stories

The Open Graph lets people who use your app share stories about the things they love to do—like reading, running or cooking. These stories appear on people’s timeline and in news feed, where friends can discover, share, like and comment on them. This helps your app get more distribution.

Before your app can publish stories on the Open Graph, you have to define objects (nouns) and actions (verbs) using the App Dashboard. These actions and objects determine what stories about your app will say, and affect how discoverable they’ll be.

Here are some tips for creating simple, clear, engaging stories with actions and objects.

1. Choosing the right actions

Actions are verbs that describe high-level things people do using your app. For example, if your app helps people monitor their fitness, you might choose actions like run or walk. If your app helps people share recipes, you might choose actions like cook or rate.

The actions you choose should:

  1. Map to the most common things people do in your app
  2. Reflect what your app is all about
  3. Create a single story for a single activity performed in your app
  4. Be written in clear, simple language

Actions for watching and reading

If your app centers on watching videos or reading articles, use our pre-defined, built-in actions: watch and read. These actions can be aggregated into rich news feed stories and reports that Facebook creates on people’s timelines.

For legal and proprietary reasons, you’ll need to comply with a few additional requirements if you use these actions.

2. Choosing the right objects

Objects are things (nouns) that people can take action on in your app, such as a recipe, a route, a city or a song. When you define an object, you define an object type – a group of items that share characteristics. Each object type you choose will have multiple instances (ex: The object book might include instances Huckleberry Finn, Great Expectations and A Clockwork Orange). You should define object types, not instances, in the App Dashboard.

For example, if your app helps people monitor their fitness, you might choose to define object types like course or route — not the Golden Gate Park course, which is too specific for a type and should be an instance. If your app helps people share recipes, you might choose object types like recipe or dish — not Kale with cranberry and pine nuts.

Defining object types that represent groups of in your app allows your stories to be aggregated in interesting ways, which can help them show more prominently on Facebook. For example, maybe people build (action) structures (object) on your app, where structures include shacks, houses and mansions (instances). If someone named Jenny Smith builds a shack, mansion and a house, you could have an aggregated story like this:

  • Jenny Smith built 3 structures on Build-O-Rama

If Jenny and 4 of her friends built shacks, you could create an aggregated story like this:

  • Jenny Smith and 3 others built shacks on Build-O-Rama

Aggregated stories are interesting because they include more information and more social context—people are particularly interested when lots of their friends do the same thing (ex: cooking the same recipe or adding multiple recipes they want to cook).

You can learn more about defining objects in our guide, Working With Multiple Objects.

3. Evaluating your sentences

Once you’ve chosen your actions and objects, double check that the resulting sentences make sense. Beware of complicated verbs or overly specific language that only a niche audience would understand.

For example, you wouldn’t want to see esoteric language like this on Facebook, unless the meaning of OMG’d and snap were clear to a broad audience of more than just power users:

  • Carl Sjogreen OMG’d (action) a snap (object) on MyCoolPhotos

Since most people understand what rated and photo mean, a better sentence might be:

  • Carl Sjogreen rated (action) a photo (object) on MyCoolPhotos

Goodreads, an app for sharing the books you read, does a great job creating simple, meaningful stories.
